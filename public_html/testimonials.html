Testimonials

Ken Mitchell
disABILITY LINK
Atlanta, Georgia

Ken Mitchell

One of our consumers who did the program is now doing part-time work in our gym, healthy eating, and working out.

She just started out going to the class, and she had a bit of fun, and now she’s in a couple classes every week on nutrition, an exercise class for people with disabilities, a stress-management class and--there is a session on food—and now she wound up going into the community and asking restaurants to display easy-to-make healthy meals that people with disabilities can make, and did a series of videos on those.

It made people aware—including me, even though I don’t like to admit it—the obvious things like the physical activity and food, but also the accessibility of things. Like preparing to live a healthy life--what are the barriers you face? We changed it so the barriers are in the beginning of the workshop. You can identify those in the beginning, and figure out perceived speedbumps, detours, or barriers that are stopping you from living a healthy life now.
​
Making people aware of healthy lifestyles, not just food or exercise, but mental health and stress management as well.

Dustin Gibson
Disability Advocates for Rights and Transition 
Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania

Dustin Gibson

For over a year, I have used the Healthy Community Living curriculum across schools, jails, and prisons. It has served different purposes in each setting and can be utilized in a myriad of ways. For example, while teaching Disability Identity to a high-school class, we followed the curriculum from start to finish, but in other discussions with the same class, we used it as a source of reference or to examine particular content more in depth.

Although it presents as a curriculum, it also serves as a information and resource hub. The material it contains has been particularly useful to the youth that I have worked with, in part, because of the relevancy and authentic representation of the creators. The online format provides the possibility to refer to material while facilitating conversation. Lastly, I've used the curriculum for course prep and research which I then taught offline. 
Gwendolyn Brown
Disability Rights & Resources
​Birmingham, Alabama

Gwendolyn Brown

I have had an opportunity to check out various helpful materials relating to healthy living. I am off and on trying to live a healthier lifestyle and some days are easier than others. However, I hadn't found anything that really caught my attention until having access to Healthy Community Living materials.

The online site as far as the layout of how each section was appropriately connecting with the other was wonderful. The opportunities to have had interactive moments with participants that were engaging caused the material to become even more meaningful and more desired to apply to one's self. 

While going through the different areas of the program, each area was very thought-provoking. I found myself truly focusing in more about my overall health. 

I saw the time together with participants turn into moments of encouragement, giving and receiving while working on personal goals that myself or others had put in place. 

Finally, it seemed as if myself and others were finally in an environment that we could be real with each other. We were able to share honestly and with such openness. 

For the first time in many years, I had actually lost weight, not just physical, but mental weights.  My medical provider was in shock and you don't understand, it has been many YEARS since I have lost any weight. I was shocked. However, I began to also lose some mental weight, meaning, I began to release some of the weights that I took ahold of that were not good for my mental peace.

When we began to learn about the speed bumps, roadblocks and detours, by breaking down each area and seeing ourselves as a whole unit, things just clicked for the first time mentally for myself and others from what I do remember others sharing during our conversations. 

Due to having participated in the program, I no longer take on the guilt of having to say no due to the importance of my mental well being.  I think about what I am eating and the quantities AND I don't drink as many sweet drinks as I use to. I think about what I am putting in my body. I am still a work in progress, but we all encouraged each other to persevere. 
Putting goals in place helped as well. Being able to see what I am aiming for. Being able to see the progress or to determine if I have put in place goals that are a bit out there and work on putting some that are more reachable for me to see the success which in itself encourages me.
​
Thank you all for so much and thank you all for truly empowering me and others.

Sharon Washington
Blue Water CIL
Port Huron, Michigan

Sharon Washington

The Blue Water Center for Independent Living provides disability advocacy services to its rural community. The Supported Services for Veterans Families (SSVF) is one of the services the Center provides. It provides rapid rehousing services to homeless veterans and homelessness prevention services to veterans facing evictions.
 
The SSVF consumer population was selected to participate in the HCL program. Men and women veterans whose ages ranged from 21 to 65 met every Friday for two hours to discuss a variety of issues such as handling stress, goal setting, handling road blocks to goal attainment, and money management. One of the most popular and well received topics was healthy eating/meal planning.
 
Many of the consumers have chronic medical conditions and healthy eating was critical to them maintaining their health. Eating healthy began with the group deciding what healthy snacks the group would consume. Fresh fruit, yogurt and fresh vegetables ranked high among the consumers. Training each other to read food labels and learning how to effectively comparison shop were other related topics the group chose to pursue.
 
Another byproduct of the group meeting weekly was the peer support system that developed among the Veterans. Planning shared transportation to the grocery store or to recreational events and monthly doctor’s visits developed.  As intake staff at the Center survey applicant needs as they apply for services, mini groups are formed and the HCL curriculum is used as a foundation for group facilitation.

​​Chanell: My name's Chanell Calhoun. I'm an Independent Living Coordinator at SEMCIL in Rochester, Minnesota. I've been working there for a little over two years now, and did the Living Well in the Community curriculum.

Rob: I'm Rob Weigel, Accessibility Specialist in Rochester, Minnesota at the center for independent living - SEMCIL. In our workshop we had consumers with all types of disabilities, and a wide variety of ages. And it actually worked very well having a mixed group of individuals in the same group.

Chanell: They all worked really well together. So, it was neat when you got to see participants helping each other out and giving each other feedback, and sharing some of their personal stories with the group and building that trust. It was very lucky, I felt.

I'd like to add - be creative. You've got the materials there, but read your group. If you need to make changes, if it's doing the sheets individually, or if it's pairing up, or if it's doing it in groups... read your participants and be flexible, be creative, let everybody know they have a choice. Ask questions so they'll participate and it's not just 'yes' and 'no' answers. Be thankful when they share their personal stories.

Rob: I agree. Getting your group as involved as you can makes a big difference. Don't get frustrated if you don't get through the whole curriculum or the whole lesson in one session, because there are days where that won't happen. As long as your group is getting involved and the group is getting the main objective of the lesson, that's all you're trying to do. 

Chanell: I'll add one more thing. You get through each module and you think, maybe, "OK, that one's done." But it's kind of cool how you when you go into the next weeks some of those previous modules coming back in. Maybe their goals change because they're really connecting and identifying where they are at in their life, and creating a goal that really makes sense.
