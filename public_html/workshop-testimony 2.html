Facilitator Testimony

Ability360 (Arizona)

Most of the consumers that took our Community Living Skills workshop have never had a true peer support system of people with disabilities. During the workshop they connected with each other through sharing stories on topics ranging from financial management and domestic abuse to dating with a disability.

--

The Healthy Community Living Technical Assistance Program (HCL-TAP) is a wonderful program to provide peer support with other facilitators.  Sharing knowledge and experience with new facilitators and watching the program grow will make a great impact for people with disabilities.  The more facilitators can provide peer support to one another the more the program will grow and touch many lives of people with disabilities to become representatives of their communities. - Leanne Murrillo


​ABLE South Carolina (South Carolina)

One of our students was able to get their high school to make it part of their IEP (read Sam's story!), so they could come to ABLE South Carolina in the afternoon as part of their day. It would be great if other places would be able to do this so students could learn independent living skills in school and learn how to use community resources. It gives people the skills they need, and people don’t always think about that. Are we preparing people to have those opportunities?

We had two participants learn to use Uber and Liftt and they took them to class and into the community as a result of the transportation lesson. This was a big deal—this was a tremendous stride for independence for both. One taught her driver how to safely load her wheelchair into the vehicle and how to provide other accommodations so she didn’t have to rely solely on paratransit. She said the class gave her the confidence to advocate for herself.

We’ve had people build stronger relationships with their family and friends because they are self-advocating as a result of the classes. They can tell their families and friends when they disagree with them or need something different for their future. When we’ve done Healthy Relationships and Information Seeking, participants learn you don’t get what you want when you’re communicating unless you are being specific. Some of them have told us they are using these skills at schools, their doctors, and in the community, whether at the library or a service provider agency.

We’re seeing things from people who previously had been letting family members or others make all their decisions for them. We’re really pleased to see the empowerment they are getting from the class. People are talking about what they want, when we weren’t seeing that before.

Another participant is working on opening a bank account to save for an adapted vehicle as a result of the Budgeting and Finance lesson. They were able to show a plan of how they are going to save money and budget.

When you see how people are rolling as a group, you can see all these awesome things. I wish I had this class when I was younger.

--

I enjoyed working with HCL TAP as it connected me with other organizations and facilitators to learn from. I noticed as the program went on that my skills enhanced and I became a more confident facilitator thanks to this program. - Sarah Sharpe​

AIM (New York)

We have a participant that comes to the classes and enjoys them but he says that he doesn't need help and he can do everything on his own; he is respectful about this and is active in the class. 

Yesterday when I was teaching the advocacy section I mentioned something about how they have to advocate for their services and he said that he doesn't like the way his services are being laid out.

At the end of class he kind of hung around and so I reminded him again that he is in charge of his services and we can help him if he wants.  He did asked for my information before he left; him just asking for our information was a huge step for him.

Working for an IL Center for the past 14 years I notice that sometimes staff become so focused on programmatic requirements that delivering these classes has aided in bringing staff back to the real reason why we are all here, the people we serve. Delivering these classes is the highlight of my week.

The classes have allowed insight into disability from multiple perspectives and has changed some negative views that one might have at the beginning of class. Having a wide range of age has been enlightening on the views that one holds on disability. When discussing the "pearls" that we sometimes over look that were created from one's disability we had an 80+ yr old woman that stated that she did not have any pearls. As the facilitator I recognized that she had a love for her staff that had been bringing her to classes and inquired with her if she would have her staff if it was not for her disability. She acknowledged that she would not have the staff in her life and recognized that they were her pearls.
 
Alliance Center for Independence (New Jersey)

I think the success story is that everyone quickly felt comfortable sharing about their disability, and experiences.  Even the gentleman who attended the session for the first time.

One of our participants in our last session of Community Living Skills and Living Well in the Community… we had a job opening and she applied and got hired at our center. She’s been working with us since January. She is working with individuals on work-readiness skills. That’s a success -- someone who graduated from the program and is now working full-time with us.

--

I really enjoyed being part of the TAP group.  I enjoyed learning about how other centers teach the HCL programs and the approach they take providing the program to their participants.  I especially enjoyed conducting my own lesson (on advocacy) and getting feedback from those in the group.  That was meaningful to me because I consider them my colleagues.  Thank you for giving me the opportunity to participate. - Luke Koppisch

Disability Network of Mid-Michigan (Michigan)

TAP has given me the opportunity to network the nation!  I have learned from my peers on their strategies and organizational missions which have helped me grow in my own role.  The content supports the mission for independent living and has given me additional tools to support our consumers in their quest to continue independence! - Ann Craig
 
Independence Center (Colorado)

One of our consumers told us about how she went to the gym a couple times in the previous week and worked out.  When asked how she felt she said she was sore but was going to continue to work out.
 
One of the participants said that from the budgeting talk last week he realized that he didn't have to spend all his money every month.  When he was younger his parents used to give him allowance and he didn't get more money until he spent all that money.  That has carried over to him as an adult and he thought he had to spend his money every month.  After last week's class he finally realized he didn't have to spend everything.
 
One participant has lost 4 1/2 pounds since we taught the self-care class a couple weeks ago and she exercises a minimum of 3 times a week.
 
One of our participants talked about that during her spare time she liked to sing and listen to music.  She got up in front of the class and sang a song.

Mounting Horizons (Texas)

Participating in the HCL (Healthy Community Living) Training was super helpful as a facilitator of ILS-[independent living skills] geared classes, not only because we got to receive input from other advocates and see their own facilitating styles, but we also got to see a myriad of ways one could apply the curriculum and tailor it to one’s audience. It was so great getting to meet fellow advocates from around the country and I look forward to utilizing the curriculum more in the future! - Ashley Newell

I so enjoyed the HCL Training… I learned so much from the program itself (Love It)… The way it is designed is easy to follow and understand and has a lot of useful information… I also loved that we got to share and hear the experiences and ideas of others… Definitely well worth my time. - Renota Shepherd


Southern California Resource Services for Independent Living (California)

The group gained a better understanding of budget and financing. The topic was not only important, but the consumers were interested in how to do a better job at saving money and the difference between the needs and the wants. Consumers also learned how to stay out of debt. The resource sheets were printed out and handed out to consumers. They were excited to receive a sheet on a monthly basis to help assist them in their budgets.
 
Time management was very open and engaging. Some of the participants opened up about having too much time on their hands and shared about getting more involved in the community, like finding resources. Participants did not always agree with the wants and the needs. Some felt theirs were both, not just one or the other. They developed their own needs and wants. Participants gained insight on how to manage their time and knowing that by structuring their time is valuable. 
 
The class was interested in learning about technology. At the same time, technology can be expensive. A quote from a consumer was very interesting. She stated, "People are not dumb. People are smart, because they are the ones that are creating the technology." The class requested a second session to learn more about power and software and what makes a computer work. The group was engaged and had an opportunity to find out about different devices.
 
We did a follow up class from last week on technical skills and found the group very engaged. Lots of questions were asked about using passwords and securing devices. A lot of the information given was new to the participants. A lot of the time was spent answering questions about how to use devices and keeping their information secure. 
 
There were a lot of conversations and the group was very energetic about transportation. The session was something all the participants could relate to. One participant shared about her commute, which it takes her 3 1/2 hours each way to get to our workshops.
